## giro-galoppo

**Verdict: MINOR**

### Victory / Game-End

Summary: "The game ends when one or more horses cross the finishing line. All players still complete their moves for that round. The player whose horse is furthest past the finishing line wins."

Source (lines 159–176): "The game is over when one or several horses cross the finishing line. All players still use the cards that they have chosen for this round and move their horses… The player whose horse is in the lead wins the game."

✓ The summary rephrases "in the lead" as "furthest past the finishing line" — this is consistent; the source example (lines 177–181) shows winner is the one who crossed farthest.

### Chain-Pushing (Unsupported claim)

Summary (Special Rules): "Pushing can chain: if the pushed horse lands on another occupied space, continue pushing back."

Source movement rules (lines 119–123): "If a horse finishes its movement on an occupied space, the horse that is already standing there is pushed back to the next unoccupied space, but never further than the starting gate."

The source says the pushed horse goes to "the next **unoccupied** space" — implying it skips over occupied spaces until it finds a free one. It does not describe a chain reaction. The summary's chain-pushing rule is unsupported.

**Assessment:** The source says pushed horse goes to "the next unoccupied space" — which effectively prevents a chain because the horse skips to the first free space, not to the next single space. The summary invents a chain-reaction mechanic not found in the source. However, whether this is MAJOR depends on whether players at the table would get a rule wrong. Players using the summary would expect a cascade (A pushes B which pushes C), whereas the source has A push B directly to the next free space without cascading. This is a genuine error but affects only a narrow edge case. Verdict: MINOR (edge case rule that would rarely arise, but is genuinely unsupported).

## gladiator

**Verdict: MAJOR**

### Prefilter Warning

The prefilter flagged `SOURCE_SUSPECT: title tokens ['gladiator'] absent from first 3000 chars`. The source begins with OCR-garbled text from an Avalon Hill rulebook; the word "GLADIATOR" appears but is garbled. Reading the first 200 lines confirms this is the correct rulebook for a gladiatorial combat game (mentions Roman arena, gladiator log sheets, secutors, Retarius, etc.). The source mismatch flag is a false alarm — source is indeed the Gladiator rulebook.

### MAJOR Finding 1: Gladiator Creation — Invented Point-Allocation System

Summary (Setup section): "Each player creates their gladiator using a pool of points: Allocate points to: Training, Strength, Agility, Constitution, Combat capabilities, and Armor"

Source (lines 89–98): "TYPE: The classification of gladiator: either light, medium, or heavy. A Retarius is considered a medium gladiator. The classification determines the armor worn, and consequently the speed of the man. Each player is free to choose the type of gladiator desired… The type must be chosen before players roll the dice to determine physical characteristics [see 7]."

Source (lines 197–219): "PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS: The numbers representing the man's relative ratings for seven essential physical characteristics are recorded here… To determine the physical characteristics, roll two dice separately on the Physical Characteristics chart. Cross index the first and second dice rolls of that chart to determine all of your gladiator's physical characteristics."

Source (line 110–114 for armor): "ARMOR: A gladiator may have various types and amounts of armor on each body area. Using the appropriate armor table for your type of gladiator, roll one die and determine the types and amounts of armor for your gladiator in all five body areas."

The summary says players "allocate points" — the source clearly states all stats are randomly determined by rolling dice on charts. There is no point-allocation system anywhere in the source. This is an invented mechanic that would fundamentally mislead players about how to create a gladiator.

### MAJOR Finding 2: Turn Structure — Alternating Turn Model is Wrong

Summary: "Players alternate turns. Each turn: 1. Movement Phase, 2. Combat Phase, 3. Record Damage."

Source (lines 30–38): "orders for movement are written for each gladiator on a combat record sheet. The gladiators are then moved simultaneously as per these written orders. Careful planning, maneuvering, and an element of luck are needed to defeat your opponent and win the game."

Source (lines 348–394): "SEQUENCE OF PLAY… The game is played in turns. Each turn is divided into eight phases. Each phase is comprised of several sub-steps… Each player writes the movement orders for his gladiator for the current phase…"

The game uses simultaneous written-order resolution across 8 phases per turn, not a simple alternating "Movement Phase → Combat Phase → Record Damage" sequence. Both players write orders simultaneously each phase and resolve them together. The summary's three-step alternating turn description is a fundamental mischaracterization of the core turn structure.

The Retarius is a medium gladiator specifically. The summary's Gladiator Types table only listing Secutor and Retiarius omits that gladiators also come in light and heavy weight classes. (MINOR omission relative to the two MAJOR errors.)
- Campaign Mode description: source (lines 1904–1910) confirms "The Campaign Game allows players to play a series of matches in which the outcome of one match can influence future events." Summary does not mention this specific condition, but the Campaign Mode section is brief in the summary and this omission is secondary to the MAJOR errors.
