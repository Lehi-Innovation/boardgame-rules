## hannibal-rome-vs-carthage

**Verdict: MAJOR**

### Finding 1 — Sudden Death conditions are vague and asymmetric condition is omitted

Summary: "Sudden Death Victory: Certain conditions can trigger an immediate victory during any turn (e.g., capturing the enemy capital or controlling a critical number of provinces)."

Source (rules 23.1–23.3):
> "23.1. The Roman player wins a Sudden Death Victory if he controls Carthago.
> 23.2.1. The Carthaginian player wins a Sudden Death Victory if he controls Roma.
> 23.2.2. The Carthaginian player wins a Sudden Death Victory if he controls all Provinces in Italia except Latium during a Victory Check phase.
> 23.3. Either player wins a Sudden Death Victory if his opponent sues for peace."

The summary collapses three distinct conditions into a vague phrase. The critical Carthaginian-only Italia condition ("all Provinces in Italia except Latium") is completely absent — that is a separate, asymmetric win path with no counterpart for Rome. The sues-for-peace trigger is also missing.

### Finding 2 — End-of-game tiebreaker omitted (scenario-dependent winner)

Summary: "If no Sudden Death occurs, victory is determined at the end of the final turn by comparing Political Points. The player controlling more provinces and key spaces wins."

Source (rule 22.4):
> "During the Victory Check phase of the last turn, the player whose Political Point total is greater wins. In the Event of a tie, the winner depends on the scenario. If there is a tie in the Hannibal scenario, the Carthaginian player wins. If there is a tie in the Hamilcar scenario, the Roman player wins."

The summary omits the scenario-specific tiebreaker entirely. A tied game played under these rules would have no resolution, and the result would be wrong for both scenarios.

### Finding 3 — Scoring section mischaracterizes Victory Check as a scoring mechanism

Summary (Player Reference): "Victory: Sudden Death (capital capture/province control) or most Political Points at game end"

The "capital capture" description for Sudden Death is misleading — the Roman Sudden Death is capturing the Carthaginian capital, not a symmetric "enemy capital" rule. The Carthaginian path through Italia provinces is not capital capture at all.

## hare-and-tortoise

**Verdict: MAJOR**

### Finding 1 — Hare movement table is structurally misleading for the 4-card case

Summary (movement table):
> "Hare | 1-3 | Advance 2 tiles"
> "Hare | 4 (and in 1st place or tied) | Does NOT advance (nap!). Exception: still on starting line = moves normally"

Source:
> "If 1 to 4 Hare cards were played, the hare advances 2 Track tiles. ...unless 4 Hare cards were played when the hare is already in first place (even just tied for first). In this case, he does not advance."

The summary's two-row structure implies that playing 4 Hare cards always results in one of the two listed outcomes — either nap (if in 1st) or... there is no third row. A reader using the table would not find the rule for "4 Hare cards played, hare is NOT in 1st place" and could not determine the correct action. The correct rule is that 4 cards + not-in-1st-place → advance 2 tiles (same as 1-3 cards), but this case is absent from the table. Players using the summary alone would likely conclude the hare never advances when 4 cards are played and it is not in 1st place.

### Finding 2 — Exact point values (5 / 3 / 2) absent from summary

Summary: "Bet on 1st place animal = highest points / Bet on 2nd place animal = medium points / Bet on 3rd place animal = fewer points"

Source: "1st place: 5 points / 2nd place: 3 points / 3rd place: 2 points"

The summary gives only relative descriptions ("highest", "medium", "fewer") with no actual numbers anywhere in the document (not in Scoring section, not in Player Reference). Players cannot score a game using the summary.

### Finding 3 — Tiebreaker omitted

Summary Scoring section: "The player with the most total points wins." (no tiebreaker)

Source: "If it is a tie, the animal that is higher on the podium is the tiebreaker. If it is still a tie, play again!"

The tiebreaker mechanism is entirely absent from the summary.
