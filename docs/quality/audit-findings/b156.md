## oriflamme-ablaze

**Verdict: MAJOR**

**Finding 1: Twin card mechanic completely misrepresented (MAJOR)**

Summary: "**Twin** | Set aside during setup. When playing a card from hand, may also play the Twin as a copy placed adjacent (same effect as the copied card)."

Source:
- Setup: "Set aside your Twin card, face up." (setup matches)
- Prince ability: "PRINCE: When you reveal your Prince, place your family's Twin, revealed, in the Queue. Gain 1 [IP]."
- Twin ability: "TWIN: Gain 1 [IP]. When the Twin is eliminated, discard your family's Prince, unless it is covered by another card."
- "The Twin can only be played through the Prince's ability."

The summary invents a completely different mechanic: Twin as a copy of another card played adjacent. In the actual rules, the Twin can only enter play when the Prince is revealed (the Prince places the Twin), not by the player playing a card from hand and choosing to copy it. The Twin gains 1 IP each round and links to the Prince (each protects the other from discard). The summary's description would mislead players into thinking the Twin works independently as a copy card.

**Finding 2: Card list in summary does not match actual Ablaze cards**

Summary lists: Cutthroat, Twin, Poisoner, Traitor, Smuggler, Duchess, Banker, Guardian, Bribery, Herald

Actual source cards visible in extracted text include: Prince, Twin (separate, Prince-linked), Queen, Cutthroat, Apothecary, Felon, Schemer, Impersonation, Plot, Trap, Bribery

Many cards in the summary (Poisoner, Traitor, Smuggler, Duchess, Banker, Guardian, Herald) have no support found in the extracted text. Searched: "Poisoner", "Traitor", "Smuggler", "Duchess", "Banker", "Guardian", "Herald" — all absent from the extracted rulebook.

Note: The extracted file is 364 lines and appears complete based on the abilities section being present. The Cutthroat and Queen do appear in the source, but most of the summary's listed cards do not match the source's cards, and some real cards (Prince, Apothecary, Felon, Schemer, Impersonation, Plot, Trap) are entirely absent from the summary's card table.

**Finding 3: Summary omits Prince card and its relationship with Twin**

The Prince is an entirely separate Character card that is key to understanding the Twin mechanic, yet neither the Prince nor its link to the Twin appear in the summary at all. This makes the summary unplayable since players wouldn't know how to deploy the Twin.

### Victory / Game-End Conditions — verified

Summary: "After 6 rounds, 1 card remains unplayed. Most Influence Points wins. IP on cards still in Queue do not count. Tiebreaker: Most cards remaining in the Queue."

Source: "Since players started the game with 7 cards in their hand, after the 6 rounds of the game, they will have 1 card left... Whoever accumulated the most Influence points wins the game! At the end of the game, any Influence points still on the cards in the Influence Queue do not count in the final score. If there is a tie, the tied player with the most cards still in the Influence Queue wins."

Match — the victory conditions are correct.

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 5, SUPPORTED: 5 (victory conditions section is fine; the error is in card mechanics)

## origin

**Verdict: MAJOR**

**Finding 1: Innovation tile scoring completely wrong (MAJOR)**

Summary: "Innovation tiles | Highest level tile scores: L1=1, L2=3, L3=6, L4=10, L5=15"

Source (END OF THE GAME section): "Each Innovation tile placed in front of a player gives him 1 point, except for the level 5 Innovation tiles that give 5 points."

The summary fabricates a progressive scoring table (1/3/6/10/15) that does not exist. The actual rule is every Innovation tile = 1 point, except L5 = 5 points. A player with 2×L1, 2×L2, 1×L3, 1×L4, 1×L5 tiles would score 11 points per the source (confirmed by the worked example: "2 level 1 Innovation tiles, 2 level 2 Innovation tiles, 1 level 3, 1 level 4 and 1 level 5 = 11 points"). Under the summary's invented table, that same set would score 1+1+3+3+6+10+15 = 39 points — a massive discrepancy. This error would make every end-game score computed from the summary incorrect.

**Finding 2: "Villages on the board: Various bonuses" has no source support**

Summary: "| Villages on the board | Various bonuses |"

Source END OF THE GAME scoring section lists only: Objective cards, cards still in hand (1 point each), Hunting tokens (face value), Strait tokens (face value), Innovation tiles. Villages are not listed as a scoring source anywhere in the game-end section. Searched: "village.*point", "village.*score", "bonus" — no match found.

This is an invented scoring source that would mislead players.

### Other Scoring Sources — verified

Summary: "Completed Objective cards | Variable (per card)." Source: "Players earn as many points as indicated on the Objective cards." Match.

Summary: "Hunting tokens | Face value (4-8 each)." Source: "The Hunting tokens and the Strait tokens give as many points as indicated on the tokens." + Components list: "Hunting tokens (Value 4 to 8)." Match.

Summary: "Strait tokens | Face value (3-5 each)." Source: Strait tokens (Value 3 to 5). Match.

No mention of cards-in-hand scoring in summary. Source: "Each Action, Permanent, and Objective cards still in hand gives 1 point each." Omission, but this is a secondary finding given the major innovation tile error.

### Tiebreaker

Summary does not mention a tiebreaker. Source: "In case of a tie, the tied player having the most cards in hand wins the game." MINOR omission, but overshadowed by the MAJOR finding.

### Game-End Triggers

Summary: "Game ends when either the Tribe Pawn reserve is empty, or no player can take a legal action."

Source lists four end triggers: "(1) One of the three cards' decks is empty (Action, Permanent, or Objective). (2) All the Innovation tiles have been taken from the Rewards Board. (3) The Tribe Pawns reserve is empty. (4) All players have used all their Villages."

Summary omits three of the four game-end triggers. This is a significant procedural omission.

### Turn Structure — verified

Summary: "MUST perform one of 3 actions, then collects Rewards. Additionally, may play up to 1 card of each color at any time during the turn."

Source: "a player MUST do one of the following 3 actions... The player takes the Rewards corresponding to his action... a player can play up to three cards, one of each color (Yellow, Orange, Purple)." Match (summary says "up to 1 card of each color" which is consistent with "one of each color").

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 12, SUPPORTED: 8 (4 innovation tile scores wrong, 1 unsupported scoring category, cards-in-hand score omitted)

## outdoor-survival

**Verdict: MINOR**

The prefilter flagged SOURCE_SUSPECT, but the extracted text is confirmed as the actual Outdoor Survival rulebook. The text begins with the Basic Game quick-start card (LOST scenario), then continues with the full Rules of Play folder covering the Life Level Index system, all five scenarios, turn sequence, terrain movement costs, etc.

The summary is broadly accurate but highly generic and omits key mechanics. Checking against the verification quota:

### Victory / Game-End Conditions

Summary table:
- "Basic | Navigate from start to a designated exit point"
- "Survival | Survive while reaching safety; manage water, food, and shelter"
- "Lost | Find your way using direction cards (simulating being lost)"
- "Full Survival | Combine all survival elements; first to reach safety wins"

The source defines five actual scenarios: LOST (get out of wilderness), SURVIVAL (traverse wilderness before opponent), SEARCH (find a lost person), RESCUE (find and guide lost party to safety), PURSUE (escapee vs. pursuer). The summary's scenario names and descriptions do not match the actual five scenarios. "Basic" is not a scenario name in the source; the Basic Game uses LOST Scenario Card No. 1. "Full Survival" is not a scenario at all in the source.

However, the scenario details are not deeply checkable without the scenario cards themselves, which describe each "TO WIN" condition separately and are not fully reproduced in the extracted text. The source does confirm the general structure (five scenarios of increasing complexity, each has a "To Win" section). The summary's scenario mapping is partially wrong (e.g., "SEARCH" is a named scenario and exists but the summary does not include it; "PURSUE" exists but is not in the summary; "Full Survival" is invented), but since the scenario cards' specific win conditions are not fully extracted, we cannot pinpoint what specific numerical/procedural errors arise from this.

**MINOR finding**: The summary invents scenario names ("Full Survival") and omits real scenarios (SEARCH, RESCUE, PURSUE). These differences would not cause players to misplay specific rules during play (each scenario card gives its own instructions), but would misrepresent what scenarios exist.

### Turn Structure

Summary: "Determine direction → Move through terrain → Check survival needs → Resolve encounters"

Source (Sequence of Play): Step 1: Select scenario, setup. Step 2: Determine turn order. Step 3: Roll Direction Ability chart, roll die, move. Step 4: Check NECESSITIES chart for food and water. Step 5: Adjust Life Level if triggered. Summary captures the essence of gameplay without major errors.

**MINOR omission**: The Life Level Index system (Food Index, Water Index counters, trigger points reducing life level) is the core survival mechanic. The summary mentions "Dehydration: Players must find water sources regularly or suffer dehydration effects leading to death" which is broadly correct but omits the formal tracking system (Life Level A through O, Food/Water Index counters). This is an omission rather than a contradicted fact.

### Components

Summary: "3 full-color interlocking, folding map boards (hex grid wilderness terrain)"

Source: "22" x 24" Mapboard of 'the Wilderness'" — the source mentions a single mapboard (one piece, not three). Searched: "3 map", "three map", "interlocking" — no match found. The summary appears to invent "3 full-color interlocking, folding map boards." The source says 1 mapboard. This could be a MAJOR error if wrong, but since the actual components are listed briefly in the source, and the source only says "22" x 24" Mapboard," the 3-board claim has no support. However, this is a components claim rather than a rules error, so it would not cause players to play the game wrong.

### Scoring

No explicit scoring numbers exist in the source (win conditions are scenario-dependent, not point-based). Summary correctly notes "Players who succumb to dehydration, starvation, or exposure are eliminated" which is consistent with source ("he has not 'survived' and is automatically out of the game").

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 2 (no numerical scoring; movement costs mentioned generically), SUPPORTED: 2

**Overall verdict: MINOR** — The summary is a superficial treatment that invents "3 interlocking map boards" and four generic scenario names that don't match the real five named scenarios, but none of these would cause specific rules to be misplayed. The Life Level system omission is significant but the summary doesn't contradict it. If player count is wrong ("2-4" in summary vs. "for more than one player" and "solo" in source), that is also a limitation.

## p-nct

**Verdict: PASS**

### Victory / Game-End Conditions — Basic Game

Summary: "Win by connecting two opposite sides of the board with a continuous chain of your visible dots (viewed from above). Dots are connected if they occupy adjacent spaces, regardless of level. Both PUNCT and minor dots count for connections. If all pieces are placed and no connection is made, the game is undecided."

Source: "The game ends when a player succeeds in connecting two opposite sides. The first player to do so wins. Look at the board from above to check whether a connection is completed or not... Each dot must be visible from above to be part of the connection. Dots are connected if they occupy adjacent spaces, no matter their level. A connection can run from any space on one side to any space on the opposite side. As far as connections go, there is no difference between a PÜNCT and a minor dot. If you put your last piece on the board and neither you nor your opponent has succeeded in completing a connection, the game ends undecided."

All claims verified. Match.

### Victory / Game-End Conditions — Standard Game

Summary: "Placement restriction: New pieces may never be placed in or partially in the central hexagon."

Source: "A new piece may never be placed directly in the central hexagon, not even partially; it must first be placed in the gaming area that encircles it. Only pieces that are already on the board can be moved into (and out of) the central hexagon!" Match.

Summary: "Tiebreaker: If neither player completes a connection when the last piece is placed, count visible dots in the central hexagon (viewed from above). The player controlling more spaces wins. Equal = draw."

Source: "If neither of the players succeeds in completing a connection, the game ends when somebody places their last piece on the board. You and your opponent both count how many spaces you control in the central hexagon (i.e. count how many dots of your color you can see from above). The player who controls the most spaces is the winner. If you and your opponent end up with the same number of spaces, the game is a draw."

Match confirmed.

Summary states the tiebreaker applies "when the last piece is placed." Source: "the game ends when somebody places their last piece on the board." Match.

### Actions / Movement

Summary: "The PUNCT (colored dot) must be moved in a straight line, any number of spaces, over other pieces, and in/out of the central hexagon."

Source: "The PÜNCT must always be moved in a straight line. You may move it over any number of spaces, over other pieces and in and out of the central hexagon." Match.

Summary: "Move a piece so its PUNCT lands on one of your own pieces (never on an opponent's piece)."

Source: "The PÜNCT of the piece must be moved to one of your own pieces... This means that a PÜNCT can never land on an opponent's piece, but the minor dots can!" Match.

Summary: "A piece with even one dot covered may not be moved or rotated."

Source: "It is sufficient to cover one dot of a piece to immobilize it. That piece may not be moved nor rotated as long as one or more of its dots remain covered." Match.

Summary: "Triangular pieces cannot bridge — they must always rest on 3 dots."

Source: "A triangular piece has no clearly defined ends, so such a piece cannot be used as a bridge. It must always be placed on 3 dots." Match.

Summary: "The end dots must be at the same level for stability."

Source: "A piece must always be in a stable horizontal position, so the dots at its 2 ends must be supported by pieces at the same level." Match.

### Numbers

- 18 pieces per player (6 straight, 6 angular, 6 triangular): Source "18 pieces: 6 straight pieces, 6 angular pieces and 6 triangular pieces." Match.
- "White pieces (3 dots each) | 18" and "Black pieces (3 dots each) | 18": Source "18 white pieces with 3 dots / 18 black pieces with 3 dots." Match.

### Exhaustion

Summary: "If all pieces are placed and no connection is made, the game is undecided." Correctly describes the undecided end state for basic game. Standard game has the central hexagon tiebreaker. No invented reshuffle/replenish rule.

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 6, SUPPORTED: 6
