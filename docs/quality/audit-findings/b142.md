## marvel-heroes

### Verdict: MAJOR

### Finding 1 — Direction reversal: villain assignment
**Summary:** "Each player also receives the archenemy of the player to their **left**."
**Source:** "Each player takes the Mastermind Villain card, figure and 3 Master Plan cards of the Nemesis of the Team of the player to his **right**."
- Reversal: the villain comes from the player on your right, not your left. This changes who controls whose villain.

### Finding 2 — Turn structure: action list for heroes vs. villains
**Summary:** Turn structure lists "Villain Phase: Each player activates the villain they control to oppose an opponent's heroes" with villain actions: Scheme, Move, Fight, Deploy minions.
**Source:** The source shows no separate "Villain Phase." Villain play is part of the Troubleshooting mechanic during the Missions phase. Villains (Mastermind cards) are played by other players during a Hero's Troubleshooting action. There is no standalone villain activation phase where the controlling player simply schemes/moves/fights.

The turn structure the summary describes (Headlines → Hero Phase → Villain Phase → Cleanup) is largely invented. The source has: Game Round Setup → Planning → Missions (five action rounds). The Archnemesis and villain card play are embedded within Missions/Troubleshooting, not a separate phase.

### Finding 3 — Scoring/Victory
**Summary:** "The player with the most victory points after a set number of rounds wins."
**Source:** "The Scenario details the victory conditions for the game. If the game ends on a specific condition or Game Round, the player with the most Victory Points is the winner. If there is a tie, the tied player who scored the Scenario Headline is the winner. Otherwise, the tied player with the most Team Power-ups in play is the winner."
The tiebreaker chain is omitted from the summary.

### Procedural claims verified:
1. Planning phase: Plot points, hero status, healing — SUPPORTED
2. Missions: five action rounds, alternating, First Player first — SUPPORTED
3. Troubleshooting: Trouble Level rolled from Threat tokens — SUPPORTED
4. Combat: hero has first Initiative — SUPPORTED
5. Story action: draw card, advance/manipulate track — SUPPORTED

### Numbers checked:
- 5 action rounds per Mission phase — SUPPORTED
- PP bonus: 1 PP per 5 VP — SUPPORTED
- 3 Master Plan cards per Nemesis — SUPPORTED
- 2 cards from Resource deck / 2 from Villain deck at start (3 each in 2p) — SUPPORTED
- Henchmen cards drawn equal to Henchmen rating in Mastermind combat — SUPPORTED

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 5, SUPPORTED: 5

## masons

### Verdict: MAJOR

### Finding 1 — Components: missing houses, palaces, wrong dice names
**Summary:** Components lists "3 dice (1 wall die, 1 tower die, 1 number die)" and does not mention houses or palaces.
**Source:** Components listed: "•60 wooden houses in 5 colors; •15 wooden palaces in 5 colors; •2 house dice; •1 tower die." There is no "wall die" or "number die." The dice are 2 house dice + 1 tower die. Houses and palaces are core scoring pieces completely absent from the summary's component list.

### Finding 2 — Turn structure: what dice determine
**Summary:** "Roll all 3 dice: The dice determine tower placement and scoring modifiers."
**Source:** "Roll dice: the player rolls all 3 dice. Depending on the result, he must next place 0, 1, or 2 towers as well as 1 or 2 houses." The dice determine tower placements AND house placements. The summary omits house placement entirely. Houses are major components (60 pieces) critical to scoring.

### Finding 3 — Game end condition: invented "scoring threshold" trigger
**Summary:** "The game ends when all walls have been placed or a scoring threshold is reached."
**Source:** "As soon as the last wall, the last tower, the last palace, or the last house is placed on the game board, the game ends." There is no "scoring threshold" trigger. The triggers are: last wall, last tower, last palace, or last house. The summary omits towers/palaces/houses as end triggers and invents a scoring threshold.

### Finding 4 — Tiebreaker missing
**Summary:** "The player with the most points wins." No tiebreaker stated.
**Source:** "The player who has the most points is the winner! If players tie with the most points, the player among them with the most cards left in his hand wins! If there is still a tie, those tied rejoice in their shared victory!"

### Finding 5 — Scoring mechanism: "play 1 or 2 guild cards" procedure
**Summary:** "Each player may play 1 or 2 guild cards from their hand."
**Source:** "each player must place 1 or 2 cards from his hand on the table… Instead, a player may discard 1 card from his hand, scoring no points, but draws 2 cards from the supply, adding them to his hand." The "discard 1 to draw 2" option (hand size increase mechanic) is absent from the summary, as is the mandatory nature (each player *must* play or discard).

### Numbers checked:
- 30 towers in 3 colors — SUPPORTED (source: "30 wooden towers in 3 colors")
- 60 guild cards — SUPPORTED
- 33 wooden walls — SUPPORTED
- Summary "5 types" of guild cards — source shows multiple card categories, not exactly 5 specified
- Penalty for rolling before placing: -1 point — SUPPORTED

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 5, SUPPORTED: 4

## mastermind

### Verdict: MAJOR

### Finding 1 — Key peg colors reversed (feedback mechanism)
**Summary:** "Black key peg: Correct color in the correct position. White key peg: Correct color but wrong position."
**Source:** "A red key peg for each Code Peg that's the right color and in the right position. A white key peg for each Code Peg that's the right color, but not in the right position."
The correct-position peg is **red** in the source, not black. The summary's component list also says "Key pegs in 2 colors (black and white)" but the source says "Code Pegs in 6 different colors, red and white key pegs." The summary invented black key pegs and misidentified the feedback colors. This would fundamentally mislead players.

### Finding 2 — Scoring: "lowest total score wins" is accurate but scoring mechanism wrong
**Summary:** "If the Codebreaker solves the code, count the number of guesses used. This is their score."
**Source:** "The Codemaker gets 1 point for each row of pegs played by the Codebreaker and players switch roles." The Codemaker scores points (not the Codebreaker), equal to rows used. Summary says the Codebreaker's score = number of guesses. This inverts who scores, though the result is equivalent — but "Multi-Round Game: the player with the lowest total score (fewest guesses) wins" is not stated in the source. The source only states the Object: "Solve your opponent's code in fewer turns than it takes your opponent to solve your code." The exact win condition for multi-round (lowest total) is not directly contradicted but also not explicitly stated.

### Finding 3 — Rows: "10 or 12" vs. source's "10"
**Summary:** "typically up to 10 or 12 rows"
**Source:** "If all 10 rows are used and the Code has not been broken…" and "The Codemaker gets 1 point for each row of pegs played by the Codebreaker and players switch roles." The source only mentions 10 rows. The "12" is unsupported.

### Finding 4 — Codemaker gets 11 points if code unbroken
**Summary:** No mention of the 11-point bonus.
**Source:** "If all 10 rows are used and the Code has not been broken, the game is over and the Codemaker gets 11 points (10 points + 1 bonus point)." This specific scoring rule is missing from the summary.

### Procedural claims verified:
1. Codemaker hides 4 pegs, can repeat colors — SUPPORTED
2. Codebreaker places 4 pegs per row — SUPPORTED
3. Key pegs placed in any order (no correspondence to specific guess pegs) — SUPPORTED (source: "The Codemaker does not put the key pegs in any particular order")
4. Play continues until code solved or rows exhausted — SUPPORTED
5. If Codebreaker proves Codemaker gave wrong info, replay, Codebreaker gets 3 points — confirmed from source (line 51)

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 6 (10 rows, 11 points, 3 points penalty, 4 pegs per guess, 4 pegs in code, 6 colors), SUPPORTED: 5 (12 rows claim unsupported)

## mechs-vs-minions

### Verdict: MAJOR

### Finding 1 — Scrap Effects: invented Fire vs. Metal distinction and invented numeric requirements
**Summary:** "Instead of programming, scrap a card for its element's immediate effect: Fire: Repair slot damage (requires 6 fire cards total); Metal: Repair system damage (requires 9 metal cards)"
**Source:** "Scrapping Fire or Metal cards allows you to Repair Damage: Discard the scrapped card and any 1 Damage Card." The source does not distinguish Fire from Metal for repair type — both repair "Damage" (any damage card). There is no "requires 6 fire cards total" or "requires 9 metal cards" rule anywhere in the extracted text. These numbers appear to be entirely invented.

### Finding 2 — Scrap Effects: Electric/Computery described vaguely/incorrectly
**Summary:** "Electric/Computery: Other immediate effects"
**Source:** "Scrapping Electric or Computery cards allows you to Reprogram your Command Line. Discard the scrapped card. Swap the content of any two undamaged slots." The summary calls this "other immediate effects" which is vague and loses the actual mechanic (swap slots). This is a core repair/reprogram system.

### Finding 3 — Player Phase sequence: "scrap it for an immediate effect" position
**Summary:** Turn step b: "Program: Place the drafted card into one of your 6 Command Line slots (or scrap it for an immediate effect)."
**Source:** "During Draft, Command Cards that you pick can be immediately scrapped instead of programmed. You may only scrap cards from a Draft." Scrapping happens during Draft (step 1), not as part of the Program step. The summary conflates the steps.

## melee

### Verdict: MAJOR

### Finding 1 — Plate armor MA = 4 (wrong; source says 6)
**Summary:** "Plate armor: MA = 4"
**Source:** "plate armor also = MA 6" (in movement section) and "Plate armor takes 5 hits/attack; wearer's MA= 6 ; - 6 to DX." (weapon table ARMOR section). The summary lists plate armor MA as 4, but both source references confirm it is 6 (same as chainmail). This would cause players to severely underestimate plate-armored figures' mobility.

### Finding 2 — Critical hit rule: roll of 3 vs 4 collapsed
**Summary:** "Critical hits: A natural roll of 3-4 on 3d6 is an automatic hit with double damage."
**Source:** "A roll of 3 always hits, regardless of DX, and does triple damage. A roll of 4 always hits, regardless of DX, and does double damage. A roll of 5 always hits, regardless of DX." The summary collapses 3 (triple) and 4 (double) into "3-4 = double," losing the triple-damage rule for a roll of 3.

### Finding 3 — Injury/falling rule mischaracterized
**Summary:** "Falling down: Figures that take 5+ damage in a single hit must roll to avoid falling."
**Source:** "A figure which takes 5 or more hits in one turn has its DX adjusted - 2 for the NEXT turn ONLY." (DX penalty, not falling). "A figure which takes 8 or more hits in one turn IMMEDIATELY falls down." (falling threshold is 8, not 5, and is automatic — no roll). There is no "roll to avoid falling" for 5+ hits. The summary invents a saving roll and uses the wrong threshold.

### Finding 4 — Auto-miss rolls: 16 omitted
**Summary:** "A roll of 17-18 is an automatic miss (and may result in dropping weapon)."
**Source:** "A roll of 16 always misses, regardless of DX. A roll of 17 always misses, and the attacker drops that weapon in his own hex. A roll of 18 always misses, and the attacker's weapon is broken." Roll 16 is also an automatic miss (though without weapon drop/break). The summary misses the roll-16 rule and oversimplifies the 17 vs 18 distinction (drop vs. break).

### Procedural claims verified:
1. Creating a figure: 8 ST, 8 DX, 8 extra points = 24 total — SUPPORTED
2. To hit: roll 3d6 <= adjusted DX — SUPPORTED
3. Death: ST reaches 0 — SUPPORTED (source: "when ST reaches zero, that figure is dead"; note: ST=1 = collapse, ST=0 = dead)
4. Figure creation minimum of 8 in each stat — SUPPORTED
5. Armor stops damage: Leather 2 hits, Chain 3, Plate 5 — SUPPORTED

### Numbers checked:
- MA: None=10 ✓, Leather=8 ✓, Chain=6 ✓, Plate=4 ✗ (source says 6)
- Leather armor: -2 DX — SUPPORTED
- Chain armor: -3 DX — SUPPORTED  
- Plate armor: -6 DX — SUPPORTED
- Large shield: -1 DX — SUPPORTED
- Shields stop hits: Large=2, Small=1 (summary says "Shields stop 1 additional hit") — MINOR discrepancy (large shield stops 2, not 1)

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 10, SUPPORTED: 8 (plate MA and shield hits wrong)

## memoir-44

### Verdict: MINOR

### Finding 1 — Terrain dice reductions oversimplified (not unit-type specific)
**Summary terrain table:** "Forest: -1 battle die; Hedgerow: -1 battle die; Town/Village: -1 battle die; Bunker: -2 battle dice"
**Source:** For Forest: "Infantry reduce the number of Battle dice rolled by 1, Armor reduce the number of Battle dice rolled by 2 and Artillery Battle dice are not reduced." Same differentiation for Hedgerows and Towns. For Bunker: "Infantry reduce by 1, Armor reduce by 2, Artillery not reduced."
The summary's single values ("-1" or "-2") do not reflect the unit-type dependent reductions. This is a real play error — an Armor attacking an Infantry in Forest should lose 2 dice, not 1.

However, since this could be considered a simplification in the Player Reference table while the core rules are otherwise accurate, and no outright wrong numbers or invented mechanics were found in the core turn sequence, scoring, or victory conditions... I am marking this MINOR because the error is in a summary table row (not the narrative), and the core rules text correctly describes the system.

### Finding 2 — Exhaustion rule: deck reshuffle
**Summary:** "If the Command card draw deck runs out, shuffle the discard pile to form a new deck."
**Source:** "If the draw deck runs out of cards, the discards are shuffled to form a new draw deck." SUPPORTED.
