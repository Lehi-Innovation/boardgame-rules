## art-society — MAJOR

**Numbers checked (15, all supported):** 116 painting tiles; 108 decor tiles; 20 bid cards per player; 4 starting bid cards; 4 prestige markers; 7 painting value tokens numbered 3-9; 4 complete wall bonus tokens; 2 score markers per player; 1 museum board; 1 auctioneer gavel; 4 player aids; auction = players + 1 paintings; assistant holds 1 tile; 2nd excess painting ends game; 2-4 players / 30-60 min. All match the source.

**Findings:**

1. **Contradicted rule (stated twice): museum exchange said to affect prestige.**
   - Summary (Actions): "Cannot Fit a Painting: Either exchange it at the museum (affects prestige values)"
   - Summary (Special Rules): "Paintings exchanged at the museum adjust the prestige markers, changing how much each painting type is worth"
   - Source: "This exchange does not affect the prestige of that painting type on the museum board" and "Paintings exchanged with the museum do not affect the prestige of that painting type."
   - Direct contradiction; would mislead players into mis-tracking prestige.

2. **Missing scoring sources.** Summary's Final Scoring lists prestige points, decor tiles, complete wall bonus, faux pas, and excess penalties — but omits two of the six scoring categories entirely:
   - Eyeline Bonus: "+3 points for each painting of the most prestigious type (x5) that is partly or fully in the light region between the eye icons" (faux pas paintings still count).
   - Exposed Corners: "loses 2 points for each corner of their wall that is not covered by a tile."
   - Also omits the concrete values: prestige rankings score 2/3/4/5 pts per painting (x2 for types never reaching the museum), Full Gallery = +5, excess painting = -2 each.

3. **Wrong bid-tie mechanism.** Summary: "Highest unique bid gets first pick; process continues down." Source: tied players reveal the card *underneath* the played card in their bidding stack (continuing down the stack) to break the tie — there is no "unique bid" rule.

4. **Prestige advancement mechanics omitted.** The summary never states that the unsold painting's value advances that type's prestige marker by that amount, nor the no-ties rule (advancing marker that would tie moves backwards to the first empty space), nor the >50 flip (+50) rule.

5. **Wrong designer (metadata).** Frontmatter: `designer: "Pius Mayr"`. Source credits: "Designed by Mitch Wallace."

**Verdict: MAJOR** — contradicted museum-exchange rule plus two omitted scoring categories.

## articulate — MINOR

**Numbers checked (11, all supported):** 500 cards; 4 playing pieces; 4-20+ players; teams of 2 = 1 describer/1 guesser; teams of 3 = 2/1; teams of 4 = 2/2 (never more than 2 describers); advanced option = 1 describer; PASS once per turn; wide green spin = ±2; narrow green spin = ±3; cannot move opponent behind START. All match the source.

**Findings:**

1. **Omitted penalty rule.** Source: "DESCRIBERS must be careful not to break any of the DESCRIPTION RULES... If they break one of these rules they forfeit the turn." The summary lists the description rules but omits the turn-forfeit penalty.
2. **Omitted spin-segment category rule.** Source: after landing on a SPIN (orange/red) segment, "On the next turn the ACTION or RANDOM entries are used as appropriate." Not in the summary.
3. **Category list unsupported by source (not an error).** Summary lists 6 categories (Object, Nature, Random, Person, Action, World). The extracted text only names OBJECT, ACTION, RANDOM and CONTROL. The list matches the published game, so this appears to be accurate external knowledge rather than an invention; noted for completeness.
4. Trivial omission: start segment is the OBJECT category.

Everything else (CONTROL with no time limit, winner takes next turn, describer change after winning a second consecutive turn, simultaneous-shout redraw, FINISH/CONTROL win condition, setup) matches the source.

**Verdict: MINOR** — small omissions (turn forfeiture, post-spin categories) unlikely to badly mislead, core play is faithful.

## artus — MAJOR

**Numbers checked (10, supported except noted):** 12 point tiles marked "50" (ok); 10 golden rings (ok); 88 cards / 22 per player (ok); 6 Knights per player, 5 with 4 players (ok); 1 Knight on the 0/50 space (ok); 3 rings on King Arthur / 1 ring on each other Prince (ok); 4 hand cards drawn 2+2 (ok); 1 plastic stand (ok); point tile at 50+ (ok); "28 playing figures: 6 Knights per color (4 colors) + 2 Princes" — internally inconsistent (6x4+2 = 26, not 28; there are 4 Prince figures, placed on the 4 crown seats — 2 silver + 2 bronze — which the summary's own setup implies). Inherited from ambiguous OCR; minor.

**Findings (multiple invented/contradicted mechanics):**

1. **"Move any Knight (yours or opponent's)" — contradicted.** Source: "he must move one of his own Knights." Knight cards never move opponents' knights.
2. **"Positive values move clockwise toward the King; negative values move away" — invented.** Source: Knight cards show forward ranges (e.g. "3-6" = move 3-6 spaces clockwise); exactly one Knight card (the 1-5 bidirectional card) permits backwards movement. There are no "negative value" cards. Same invention repeated for King cards in the Player Reference table.
3. **Displacement direction — contradicted.** Summary: occupant is pushed "to the next empty seat in the direction of movement." Source: displaced figure "must move to the next empty space in counter-clockwise direction" — always, even when displaced by a backwards move.
4. **Movement scoring — contradicted.** Summary: "Knights score based on their current seat's value." Source: "The value of the space that the Knight leaves (not the space the Knight lands on!) determines how many prestige points the player receives / loses."
5. **Suckling pig scoring trigger — invented.** Summary: "When a Knight lands on or passes through the seat with the suckling pig (crown plate), scoring occurs for that Knight." No such rule exists; the suckling pig is just the handle used to rotate the table, and the crown plate counts 0 points.
6. **End-game final scoring — invented.** Summary: "Final scoring: all Knights score their current seat positions." Source has no final position scoring; the game simply ends after 16 rounds (all cards played) and the highest prestige total wins. ("Score all Knights" exists only as an advanced-game scoring card.)
7. **Ring effect — invented.** Summary: "Rings on a figure make it worth more during scoring (each ring = additional points)." Source: rings only track crowning — a Prince receiving its 3rd ring immediately becomes the new King (old King keeps 1 ring); adding a ring scores prestige equal to the value of the space that Prince occupies.
8. **"Add a ring" action omitted/misrepresented.** One of the three beginner-game actions (played with 4 of the 8 King cards) is missing; the summary presents King cards as only moving the King, while the numbered King cards actually move "a correspondingly coloured Prince or the King" (colour-binding), and moving a Prince scores the space it leaves (King always 0).
9. **Initial Knight placement omitted.** Source: before play, knights are placed one at a time onto empty seats in counter-clockwise order (5 placement rounds with 2-3 players, 4 with 4 players). The summary never explains how knights get on the board.
10. Minor: "game ends when the draw piles are exhausted" — source: game lasts exactly 16 rounds (players keep playing hand cards after piles empty). Advanced game specifics (2 cards/turn, 6 hand cards, 11 rounds, 6 scoring cards with -15/-25/-50 penalties) are glossed as "additional strategic options" — acceptable brevity, but nothing usable remains.

Supported items: rotating table follows King/crown plate; displaced figures score no points; point tiles for 50+; tie = multiple winners (implied).

**Verdict: MAJOR** — core movement, displacement, and scoring rules are contradicted, and several scoring mechanics are invented outright.

## asara — MAJOR

**Numbers checked (all numbers present in the summary; it contains few):** 4 rounds/years (ok); 5 colours brown/green/red/black/white (ok); 2 cards face down when unable to follow suit (ok); tower minimum = 1 base + 1 turret, any number of trunks/windows, single colour (ok); 2-4 players (ok). The summary omits nearly every concrete number in the source (building plots 1-7 Asari, 5 Asari coloured building, 3/5 Asari bribery, 20 Asari per new year, 45 buyer cards, final-eval values 4/2-12/6, 12/6, 1 PP per 10 Asari).

**Findings:**

1. **Invented scoring bonus.** Summary: "Bonus for towers in the most different colors." No such bonus exists anywhere in the source.
2. **Scoring structure largely wrong/missing.**
   - Omits in-game build scoring: "the player receives prestige points in proportion to the number of tower sections he has used during his turn."
   - Omits the end-of-year evaluation after each of the 4 years: 1 PP per tower, 1 PP per gold-decorated section, 1 PP for the Caliph's patronage.
   - Final evaluation misstated: "Points for each completed tower based on height" — actually only the highest and second-highest tower *per colour* score (brown 4/2, green 6/3, red 8/4, black 10/5, white 12/6), plus highest/2nd-highest tower overall, 12/6 for largest/2nd-largest number of towers, and 1 PP per 10 Asari (omitted). Gold decorations and patronage score nothing in the final evaluation.
3. **Omitted actions/areas.** The "Possible Moves" table omits three standard-game board areas: Bribery at the House of Spies (pay 3 or 5 Asari, search a top-up pile, buy a section at normal cost), the Caliph's patronage space (take the tile, become next year's start player, 1 PP/year), and the coloured building next to the bank (any buyer face down for 5 Asari).
4. **Follow-suit overstated.** Summary: "Following suit applies to ALL areas (bank, building circle, and all market areas)." Source: true for all areas *except* the coloured building — "In contrast to all the other areas, players do not have to follow suit in this space." (Cards there are also stacked, face down.)
5. **Buyer cards misdescribed.** Summary setup: "Each player takes a screen and buyer cards of their color." Source: there are 45 shared buyer cards that are shuffled and "re-distribute[d]... according to the number of players" each year; card colours are suits used for follow-suit (e.g. "Since he doesn't have a blue buyer card..."), not player ownership colours. As written, the summary's setup makes the follow-suit rule impossible.
6. **Building circle mechanics omitted.** 7 plots costing 1-7 Asari; you must use exactly as many tower sections as the plot's cost (next-highest vacant plot allowed at higher price/fewer sections); pay the Asari; sections placed may extend completed towers but parts may never be swapped between towers. Summary reduces this to "Build a tower using collected sections."
7. Unverifiable: "Youngest player starts" and the price ordering brown→white in the Player Reference are not in the extracted text (setup/prices live on the crib sheet/screens, not extracted). The price ordering is consistent with the per-colour scoring scale; not counted as an error.
8. Supported: same-colour sections cost the same regardless of type; gold sections cost the same as plain; market refilled from top-up piles each year; professional version exists (recruit buyers, house of lighted windows — glossed).

**Verdict: MAJOR** — invented "most colors" bonus, the actual three-layer scoring system is missing or wrong, and several standard actions are omitted.

## asia-engulfed — MAJOR

**Numbers checked (the summary is nearly number-free; all of its numeric claims checked):** 2 players (ok); theatre/years 1941-1945 (ok); "each game turn represents approximately 3-4 months" — WRONG (source: "Game turns generally represent two months of historical time"); campaign start Dec 1941 (plausible; scenarios are in the un-extracted Play Book); 360-540 min playtime (frontmatter, not in source). Source numbers absent from the summary: 134 wooden blocks, 24 dice, 228 square + 68 round markers, 20 max bomber points, 12+/6+/3-5 VP thresholds, Jul/Aug 1945 end turn, 5 WERPs + 1 oil for Honshu, etc.

**Findings:**

1. **Wrong turn length.** Summary: "approximately 3-4 months." Source: two months per game turn.
2. **Invented turn structure.** Summary gives a 6-phase generic sequence — "Strategic Phase, Initiative Determination, Operations Phase (players alternate), Supply Phase, Production Phase, End Phase." The source has no initiative determination and no alternating operations: each game turn is a complete Japanese Player-Turn (1.11 Naval Movement → 1.12 Initial Supply Determination → 1.13 Production → 1.14 Air & Naval Combat → 1.15 Operational Movement → 1.16 Strategic Movement → 1.17 Ground Combat (Initial + Breakthrough) → 1.18 Rebasing → 1.19 Final Supply Determination), then the Allied Player-Turn repeats those phases, then a Turn Advancement Phase. Japan always moves first. The summary's Player Reference table repeats the invented structure.
3. **Invented components.** Summary lists "Cards (event and combat)." The component list contains no event or combat cards — only "One Getting Started Card" and "Two Player-Aid Cards." It also omits the Battle Board, two Air Commitment Displays, 134 blocks, and 24 dice.
4. **Victory conditions misrepresented; VP system omitted.** Summary: Japan wins by "maintain[ing] a strong defensive perimeter long enough to force Allied surrender (checked at various turn intervals)"; Allies by "recapture key objectives... or force Japan's surrender." Source (10.3): Japanese Decisive Victory = control Hawaii, OR control 12+ supplied VP areas/sea-zones, OR unconquered at end of Jul/Aug 1945 with 6+ supplied VP areas and no Allied-controlled strategic air base areas; Operational = 6+ VP areas at game end; Marginal = 3-5. Allied: Marginal = Japan holds 2 supplied VP areas, Operational = 0-1, Decisive = Japan conquered (Honshu enemy-controlled) before game end. There is no surrender mechanic and no periodic victory checks other than the automatic-victory check each Turn Advancement Phase. The entire Victory Point area system is absent from the summary.
5. **Core systems omitted.** WERPs (the production currency around which the Production Phase and strategic warfare revolve) and Special Actions (section 7.0 — required for amphibious invasions, breakthrough combat, extended naval ops, US code breaking, etc.) are never mentioned. Transport points/capacity likewise.
6. **Overstated:** "Submarine Warfare: Both sides conduct submarine campaigns against shipping." The strategic submarine campaign against shipping/production is US-only (2.0: "Strategic Warfare represents the USA's attacks on Japan's production capability via submarines and strategic bombers"); Japan has a submarine unit usable in naval combat only.
7. Minor: "B-29 operations" — the source speaks of bomber points/strategic air war from strategic air bases "within strategic bomber range of Japan"; B-29 is never named. Gloss is directionally right.
8. Supported: fog-of-war blocks ("with the information facing away from your opponents", line 725); Allied side = USA/Commonwealth/China/Philippines/DEI; Japanese fleet moves consume oil points; China-specific rules exist (9.4); combinable with Europe Engulfed.

**Verdict: MAJOR** — wrong turn length, invented turn sequence (initiative phase, alternating operations), invented card components, and the actual VP-based victory conditions are missing.
