## combat-commander-europe — MAJOR

**Verdict: MAJOR** — The summary invents a turn/initiative structure that directly contradicts the source's alternating-turn sequence of play, invents a game-end condition ("overwhelming VP advantage"), omits one of the game's three VP sources (Exit VPs) and the Surrender auto-loss system entirely, misstates the Melee result and broken-unit rules, and presents an invented "faction deck strengths" table with no basis in the source. The summary is also unusually vague: it contains almost no concrete numbers, omitting hand sizes, elimination VP values, stacking limits, and all component counts.

### Findings

1. **MAJOR — Invented turn structure / misrepresented Initiative system.**
   - Summary (Turn Structure): "The game does not use traditional alternating turns. Instead, the player with initiative plays cards and takes actions until they choose to pass or play a specific card. Then initiative may shift." Also: "After each action, the active player may continue or pass initiative." Player Reference: "Pass initiative or continue."
   - Source (5. Sequence of Play, l.750-761): "A game of Combat Commander begins with one of the players... performing the first 'Turn'. When he is finished – and draws up to his Hand Size [1.1] – the opposing player takes his first Turn. **These Turns are then alternated** until a Sudden Death Roll or other occurrence [6.3] ends the game. A Turn consists of a player choosing to either: perform one or more Orders by playing cards from his hand [O14]; or discard any number of Fate cards [O15]."
   - The Initiative card has nothing to do with turn order. Source (9.1, l.1156-1172): the Initiative card lets its holder cancel and re-roll the last die roll, passing the card to the opponent; (9.2) it breaks ties at game end. "Pass" in the source is the discard Order (O15 "Pass (discard)", l.21), not passing initiative.
   - Impact: this misleads players about the game's most fundamental structure — alternating turns capped by Order Capability (1-6 Orders per turn, l.765-770, l.731-733), with hand refill at end of each turn (5.4). The summary also omits that the inactive player can play Actions during the opponent's turn (5.2, l.777-785; Glossary l.108-111: "Actions may be played during either player's Turn"), one of the game's defining mechanics.

2. **MAJOR — Invented game-end condition; Surrender loss condition omitted.**
   - Summary (Scoring): "The game ends when: The time track runs out... One player achieves overwhelming VP advantage."
   - Source (6.3, l.880-892) lists exactly four endings: (1) a player must place an eliminated unit on the Casualty Track space holding his Surrender marker; (2) a player's last unit on the map is eliminated; (3) a player's last unit voluntarily exits the map; (4) a Sudden Death Roll less than the Time marker's space number. No VP-advantage ending exists anywhere in the source.
   - The Surrender mechanism (4.2 Casualty Track, l.705-726; 6.3.1: surrendering player "immediately and automatically loses the game regardless of the VP total") is a core system the summary never mentions.

3. **MAJOR — Exit VPs (one of the three scoring sources) omitted.**
   - Summary lists only: eliminating units, controlling objectives, events.
   - Source (7, l.916-923): "VPs are earned in one of three ways: Elimination of enemy units; **Exiting of friendly units off the opponent's map edge**; control of mapboard Objectives." Detailed in 7.2/7.2.1 (l.938-967: exit at MP cost 1, unit's VP value awarded, unit returns later as reinforcement) and 7.2.2 (forced retreat off own edge = eliminated, opponent gains VPs). The Overview/Introduction also names exiting as a victory measure (l.61-68). A player using the summary would never know exiting scores points.

4. **MAJOR — Melee result wrong.**
   - Summary: "Loser's units are eliminated **or broken**."
   - Source (O16.4.3, l.1531-1537): "The side with the lower Melee Total has **all of its participating units eliminated**. In case of a tie, both sides are eliminated unless one player began the Melee in a Bunker [F101] or Pillbox [F104]..." Melee losers are never merely broken. (The Melee procedure itself — both sides total FP plus rolls, l.1506-1523 — is correctly described, including +1 per boxed-FP unit being omitted as trivial drift.)

5. **MAJOR — Broken-unit rules contradicted.**
   - Summary: "Broken units cannot attack and must retreat; they can be rallied with Recover orders."
   - Source: Glossary (l.123-124): "A broken unit can perform most abilities, albeit at a reduced capacity." The Fire Attack example (l.1879-1881) explicitly has a broken Squad firing: "The Light MG in K8 can't fire, as it is manned by a broken unit [11.1]. **The broken Squad can fire up to 3 hexes away**, however..." Broken units cannot fire *Weapons* (3.2.3, l.622-623; 11.1, l.1313-1314), but they can attack. They also do not "must retreat": they retreat only if a Rout Order is played and the Rout Roll exceeds their Morale (O23.2, l.2141-2149); a roll below Morale has "no effect and the unit remains where it is." Recover rallying broken units is correct (O22).

6. **MAJOR — Invented faction deck characterizations.**
   - Summary (Special Rules + Player Reference table): "Russian deck has more Fire cards; German deck is more balanced"; "American: Good support, **air power**"; "Russian: More fire cards, aggressive."
   - Source: the only deck description is that there are three 72-card sets, one per nation (l.280-283). Nothing in the extracted text describes deck composition differences or national strengths. "Air Support" exists only as Event E43 (l.2615-2626), a random event that breaks units in a random hex — not an American capability. The entire Player Reference faction table is unsupported.

7. **MINOR — "Sudden Death triggers check each time marker advances."**
   - Source (6.2.2, l.863-874): a Sudden Death Roll is made only when the Time marker advances **into or beyond the space occupied by the Sudden Death marker**, not on every advance. Earlier Time! triggers just advance the marker and run the six-step procedure (6.1.2).

8. **MINOR — Components list partly wrong/vague.**
   - "Terrain overlays" listed as a component and "Wire, mines, and trenches can be placed as terrain overlays" — the source's contents list (l.91-100: six back-printed maps, 352 large + 280 small counters, 220 cards, rulebook, playbook, Track Display, four player aids) contains no overlays. Wire/Mines/Trenches are Fortification markers (F100-F106, l.3345), one per hex (8.1.3). Mechanically close (they are placed during setup or via Events/Actions), but they are counters, not overlays.
   - Setup step "Place objective markers on designated hexes" — the five Objectives are printed on each map (2.3, l.533-538); what players place are Control markers on them and Objective chits in the Objective Box (7.3.2/7.3.3), with secret chits a notable omitted feature.
   - All component counts omitted (not wrong, just absent).

9. **MINOR — Fire Resolution step 3 ("Draw a Fate card for the attack; compare to defense") omits that the defender also rolls.** Source (O20.3, l.1808-1819): attacker FP + Fire Attack Roll = Attack Total; **each defending unit adds Morale (+Cover/Command) to its own Fire Defense Roll** = Defense Total; break if Attack > Defense; tie = Suppressed (or break if Moving). The summary's outcome list (no effect, suppression, breaking, elimination) is supported (elimination = breaking an already-broken unit, 3.2.4).

### Mechanics verified correct
- Card-driven WWII tactical infantry combat, Axis (Germany) vs Allies (America/Russia) framing; Orders played on own turn from hand; Events only off the top of the draw pile via die triggers (1.7, 1.9.1.1) — summary's "Events are triggered by specific numbers on drawn cards" is an acceptable paraphrase of die triggers.
- Order list (Move, Fire, Advance, Rout, Recover, Artillery Request) matches O16-O23; Artillery Request is indeed an Order (O18) tied to Radios.
- Leaders enhancing nearby units (Command radius/stat bonuses, 3.3.1) supported.
- Sudden Death making the end time unpredictable; reshuffle at Time advance; drawing the last card of the deck advances Time (6.1.2) — summary's "discarding and reshuffling can trigger events" is loose but gestures at real deck-cycling pressure.
