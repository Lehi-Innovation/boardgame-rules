## monster-my-neighbor

**Verdict: MAJOR**

### Finding 1 — Monster special abilities table entirely scrambled

The summary's Monster Special Abilities table assigns abilities incorrectly to four of the five monsters. The source text lists effects before the card name (confirmed by cross-reference with the FAQ which unambiguously defines each monster's ability). The actual mapping is:

| Monster | Source ability | Summary claims |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Vampire | Take 1 additional point if you win (3 VPs total) | Gain +1 VP if you win (3 VP total) ✓ |
| Werewolf | You can play this card if 2 Hunter cards have been played | Hunter cards have no effect if Monster is your last card ✗ |
| Goomiho | Hunter cards have no effect if this card is the last card in your hand | First Hunter card played has no effect on you ✗ |
| Mermaid | First Hunter card played has no effect on you | Can be played if 2 Villager cards have been played ✗ |
| Mandrake | You can play this card if 2 Villager cards have been played | Can be played if 2 Hunter cards have been played ✗ |

Source (lines 60–72, effects listed then name):
> "Take 1 additional point if you win (3 VPs total). Vampire / You can play this card if 2 Hunter cards have been played. Werewolf / Hunter cards have no effect if this card is the last card in your hand. Goomiho / First Hunter card played has no effect on you. Mermaid / You can play this card if 2 Villager cards have been played. Mandrake"

FAQ confirms (lines 174–181):
> "does the Mermaid lose her ability? No, the Mermaid can still ignore the first Hunter card which is played"
> "Does the Werewolf count discarded Hunter cards to use his ability? No, the Werewolf counts only played Hunters and not discarded ones."

This confirms: Werewolf = "play condition based on 2 Hunter cards played"; Mermaid = "ignore first Hunter card played." The summary has these reversed and also swaps Goomiho and Mermaid, and swaps Mermaid and Mandrake.

A player using the summary's table would have Werewolf behaving like Goomiho, Goomiho like Mermaid, Mermaid like Mandrake, and Mandrake like Werewolf.

## monsterpocalypse

**Verdict: MAJOR**

### Finding 1 — Turn structure fundamentally misrepresented

Summary: "A player's turn has two phases: 1. Unit Turn: Spend action dice to activate and move units, attack with units, or secure power zones. 2. Monster Turn: Spend action dice to move your monster, attack, use special abilities, or perform power attacks."

This implies both phases happen every turn. The source contradicts this directly:

Source (lines 666–671): "Monsterpocalypse is played in turns, alternating between the two players. Each player starts the game by taking a Unit Activation. Then for the rest of the game, you can choose to take either a Monster Activation or a Unit Activation on your turn."

Source (lines 1245–1258): "On your turn, you must choose to perform either a Monster Activation or a Unit Activation... After you finish your Monster Activation or Unit Activation, your turn ends, and the next player takes a turn."

The player chooses ONE type of activation per turn, not both. This is a fundamental misrepresentation of how the game is played.

### Finding 2 — "Roll action dice at the start of each phase" — invented mechanic

Summary: "Players roll action dice at the start of each phase. The results determine which actions are available. Dice can be allocated to movement, attacks, or special abilities."

There is no "roll at the start of each phase" mechanic in the source. Action Dice form pools (Monster Pool and Unit Pool) that are SPENT (not rolled) to take actions. Rolling occurs only for attacks.

Source (lines 313–316): "Players use their dice as a form of currency. Sometimes you spend dice and sometimes you roll them."
Source (lines 354–361): "During the game, you move Action Dice between your Monster Pool and your Unit Pool. After you roll or spend dice from your Unit Pool, you place them in your Monster Pool. After you spend dice from your Monster Pool, you place them in your Unit Pool."

No support found for roll-at-start-of-phase (searched: "roll.*start", "start.*phase.*roll", "phase.*roll.*dice").

### Finding 3 — "Screen" action is invented

Summary: "Screen: Units can protect your monster or block enemy movement."

Source: No mention of "screen" or "screening" as an action (searched: "screen", "protect.*monster", "block.*enemy.*movement"). No support found.

The game's actual unit action list from source (Unit Activation phases, lines 674–681): Spawn Phase, Advancement Phase, Attack Phase, Push Phase — no "Screen" action.

### Verification of other claims

- **Win condition**: Summary: "Win by defeating the opposing monster (reducing it to 0 health)." Source (lines 1399–1400): "A player wins the game when all of their opponent's monsters are destroyed." The "0 health" framing is essentially correct; destroying = losing all health. The summary omits the early-end tiebreaker (most monsters remaining; then least damage taken; then draw), but the core win condition is captured.
- **Two forms (alpha and hyper)**: Source lines 244–247: "A monster begins the game in its alpha form and transitions to its hyper form after taking a certain amount of damage." ✓
- **Power zones**: Source lines 1093–1097: "Each time you power up, you gain one Power Die for each power zone held by one of your units." ✓ (summary's "power zones generate additional action dice" is broadly correct though they generate Power Dice specifically, not Action Dice)
- **Buildings destructible**: Source lines 1010+: buildings can be attacked and destroyed ✓
- **Thrown objects can damage other figures**: Source confirms throw is a power attack type (line 1567) ✓
- **Stomp**: Summary lists "Stomp" as a separate monster action, but source says stomp is a power attack type (line 1566–1567: "power attacks include body slam, ram, rampage, stomp, swat, and throw"). Minor conflation but not direction-reversing.

### Exhaustion rules

No deck/supply exhaustion mechanic in this game. Unit reserves: "When units are destroyed, they are returned to your unit reserves to be spawned again later." (source line 274–277). Not applicable.

NUMBERS_CHECKED: 0 relevant numbers in scoring (victory is condition-based), SUPPORTED: N/A

## mord-im-arosa

**Verdict: PASS**

### Victory & End Conditions

Summary: "The game ends when: Any player has 10 clues on the investigation sheet, OR Any player runs out of supply cubes."
Source (lines 186–188): "The game ends as soon as there are 10 clues of one player on the investigation sheet, or as soon as one player has no clues left in his supply." ✓

Summary: "The player with the most suspicion points is the culprit. The player with the fewest suspicion points wins."
Source (lines 212–216): "The player with the most suspicion points is convicted as the culprit. The player with the least suspicion points may congratulate himself as the winner. There can be several culprits and several winners." ✓

### Scoring table

Summary:
| On a crime scene floor | 3 |
| Adjacent to a crime scene | 2 |
| Any other floor | 1 |

Source (lines 197–202): "Each clue lying at a crime scene counts as 3 suspicion points. Each clue adjacent to a crime scene counts as 2 suspicion points. Every other clue counts as 1 suspicion points." ✓

### Procedural claims — Act I

Summary: "No victim found: Remove any clues found, close the tower, throw all removed clues back into the shaft one by one. Then throw 1 additional cube from your supply into the shaft (sloppy investigation penalty)."
Source (lines 57–66): "if the investigator finds clues, he takes these out. Then he puts the lifted parts of the building on again and throws all clues he took out back into the shaft, one after another. Sloppy investigation: since the investigator looked for a victim in the wrong place, now he himself leaves an additional clue: he throws (another) one of his own clues from his supply into the shaft." ✓

Summary: "Victim found: Place the victim on the matching floor space of the investigation sheet. For each opposing player's clue found on that floor, that player places 1 cube from their supply on the investigation sheet at that floor. The investigator ignores their own clues."
Source (lines 67–111): ✓ matches in substance.

### Act II — Suspect

Summary correctly describes: name suspected players, expose one floor, each suspected player with clues there places 1 cube from supply on investigation sheet per clue, remove clues and re-drop them, for each wrongly accused player (no clues) investigator drops 1 cube. Source lines 127–138 ✓.

### Act II — Cover Your Tracks

Summary: "Expose a floor. Count your own clues there. Remove up to that many of your cubes from the investigation sheet and return them to your supply."
Source (lines 177–184): "The investigator exposes a floor of his choice. He counts his own clues there. Then he may remove as many of his own clues (not more) from the corresponding floor on the investigation sheet as he has found on the exposed floor of the hotel tower, and put them back into his supply." ✓ (the source specifies removal is from "the corresponding floor on the investigation sheet" — the summary says "the investigation sheet" which is slightly imprecise but not misleading for gameplay)

### Ground floor special rule

Summary: "To investigate the ground floor, at least one of your own clues must be present there. If not: no clues from suspects go on the investigation sheet, and you throw 1 cube per suspected player into the shaft."
Source (lines 161–170): ✓ matches.

### Exhaustion rules

Summary says: no exhaustion rule stated. No deck/bag exhaustion in this game (cubes are individual supply). Source line 189–191: "It may happen towards the end of the game that a player has to place more clues on the investigation sheet than he has left in his supply. In this case, he takes the clues required out of the exposed floor of the hotel." The summary captures this at line "If a player must place more clues on the investigation sheet than remain in their supply, take the needed clues from the exposed hotel floor." ✓
