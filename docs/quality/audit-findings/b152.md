## nexus-ops

### Prefilter: OK

### Verdict: MAJOR

### Findings

**FINDING 1 — Combat system entirely wrong (invented mechanic / procedure reversal)**

Summary: "Attacker and defender alternate rounds. Each round: roll dice for each unit group (by type, in ascending cost order). Defender may retreat after any round."

Source (lines 327–335): "Units attack in a specific order, known as the battle order. This order is indicated on the reference sheet and runs from right to left – with the most powerful units attacking first, and the least powerful attacking last. In other words, all Rubium Dragons attack first, then all surviving Lava Leapers attack, followed by all surviving Rock Striders, and so on. Each unit rolls only one attack during battle. After each unit attacks in order, the battle is over – even if both the attacker and defender have units remaining in that contested hex."

Source (lines 337–339): "When a unit type attacks, both the attacking player and the defending player simultaneously roll for each of their units of that type."

Three distinct errors in the combat description:
1. The summary says attacker and defender alternate rounds — the source says both sides roll simultaneously for each unit type, and there is exactly one pass through the battle order; the battle ends after all unit types have rolled once.
2. The summary says unit groups roll in "ascending cost order" — the source says most-powerful (Rubium Dragon) attacks first (descending power / ascending combat value, not cost).
3. The summary invents a "retreat after any round" mechanic — the word "retreat" does not appear anywhere in the extracted source.

**FINDING 2 — Tiebreaker rules entirely missing**

Summary (Scoring section): "First player to reach 12 or more victory points wins immediately. VP come from Battle Victory cards and completed Secret Mission cards." No tiebreaker stated.

Source (lines 471–485): "The player with the highest number of victory points among the remaining players wins the game. In case of a tie, the tied player with the most controlled hexes wins. If there is still a tie, the tied player with the most units on the board wins. If there is still a tie, the tied player with the most rubium tokens wins the game. Finally, if there is still a tie, the game ends in a draw."

This four-tier tiebreaker applies when a player is eliminated and VP are compared among survivors. Its complete omission would produce incorrect game-end outcomes.

## notre-dame

### Prefilter: OK

### Verdict: MAJOR

### Findings

**FINDING 1 — Hotel action description is wrong (wrong action assigned to sector)**

Summary (Actions table): "Hotel | Gain 1 prestige per message in other players' boroughs"

Source (lines 305–312): "Hotel: Players executing this action place one influence marker from their personal supply onto the hotel sector of their borough. If a player now has 1 or 2 influence markers in the hotel, the player may either take 1 gold coin from the general supply, or take 1 influence marker from the general supply, or move their rat marker back 1 space on the rat track. If the player has 3 or more influence markers in their hotel, the player gets two bonuses (either taking two different bonuses, or the same one twice)."

The "messages" effect belongs to the Carriage House action (carriage collects messages from marketplaces). The Hotel gains gold coins, influence markers, or rat track reductions — not prestige from messages. A player using the summary at the table would gain the wrong resource from Hotel actions.

**FINDING 2 — Park action description is wrong (Trusted Friend's effect listed under Park)**

Summary (Actions table): "Park | Place influence in any sector"

Source (lines 447–457): "Park: Players executing this action place one influence marker from their personal supply onto the park sector of their borough. Players then move their rat marker one space back on the plague track (regardless of the number of influence markers in the park). Having influence markers in the park will add bonus points to the number of prestige points a player gets during the game. For each 2 (rounded down) influence markers in the park, a player earns 1 additional prestige point every time the player does something in the game to earn prestige points."

Source (Trusted Friend, lines 320–326): "Players executing this action place no influence markers. Instead, players place their trusted friend in any of the seven sectors in their borough. Next, players execute the action associated with the sector on which the trusted friend sits, just as though the player had placed an influence marker there."

"Place influence in any sector" describes the Trusted Friend action, not Park. Park moves the rat marker back and grants ongoing bonus prestige per 2 markers — the summary omits both effects entirely.

**FINDING 3 — Hospital action description is wrong (wrong scale for rat reduction)**

Summary (Actions table): "Hospital | Move rat marker back 1 per influence in hospital"

Source (lines 473–479): "Hospital: Players executing this action place one influence marker from their personal supply onto the hospital sector of their borough. Players then move their rat marker one space back on the plague track (regardless of the number of influence markers in the hospital)."

The "1 per influence" claim is wrong. Playing the Hospital card always moves the rat back exactly 1 space, regardless of how many markers are in the hospital. The number of markers in the hospital does reduce the plague effect in Phase 5, but the immediate action is always 1 space. A player with 3 markers in the hospital who plays the card would (wrongly, per summary) move back 3 spaces on the spot.

**FINDING 1 — Turn structure has an invented step (structural error)**

Summary presents 6 turn steps:
1. Draw Cards and Resolve Secrets
2. **Shift Cards** (described as: "The first face-down card moves to the face-up position. The second face-down card shifts to first position.")
3. Modify Deterrents
4. Place a Card
5. Resolve Face Up Card
6. End Turn

Source presents 5 steps:
1) Draw Cards and Resolve Secrets (includes the card shift internally: "At this time, the card in your first face down card location automatically moves to your face up location, with the card in the second face down location moving up to the first.")
2) Modify Deterrents
3) Place a Card
4) Resolve Face Up Card
5) End Turn

The "Shift Cards" is not a separate named step in the source — it is an automatic transition that occurs at the end of step 1. The summary invents it as step 2, which could mislead players about when they can modify deterrents (they might think they can modify deterrents BEFORE the shift, when in fact the shift is already done before step 2/Modify Deterrents begins). However, since the card mechanics work out the same and this is procedural framing rather than a rule that produces wrong outcomes, this is MINOR.

## nuns-on-the-run

### Prefilter: WARN SOURCE_SUSPECT (title tokens absent from first 3000 chars; confirmed correct source after reading: text describes novices, abbey, Abbess and Prioress, Fréderic Moyersoen as designer — correct game)

### Verdict: MAJOR

### Findings

**FINDING 1 — Movement distances entirely wrong**

Summary (Actions table and Player Reference):
- Sneaking: 1 space
- Walking: 2 spaces
- Running: 3 spaces

Source (lines 231–238):
- "Standing Still — did not move"
- "Sneaking – moved 1 or 2 dots"
- "Walking – moved 3 or 4 dots"
- "Running – moved up to 5 dots"

The summary understates walking (2 vs 3-4 dots) and running (3 vs up to 5 dots) by a large margin. The movement example confirms running = 5 dots: "she runs— moving 10 dots to 88" (on turn 1, with 2 movement phases = 5+5=10). Players using the summary would move far too slowly as novices, fundamentally breaking the game's pacing.

**FINDING 2 — Guard win condition is wrong**

Summary: "Guards win: If no novice completes their mission by end of turn 15, or if all novices are caught."

Source (lines 282–283): "You win if you accumulate capture points equal to the number of players. You also win if no novice has won by the end of the 15th turn."

Source (lines 631–634): "The GUARDS win if they accumulate capture points greater than or equal to the number of players (e.g., 6 capture points in a 6-player game). They also win if no novice wins by the end of the 15th turn of the game."

The "all novices are caught" condition in the summary is invented. Guards win by accumulating capture points (catching any novice multiple times counts) equal to the number of players — not by catching all novices once. In a 6-player game, catching 1 novice 6 times wins for the guards; in a 2-player game, catching 1 novice twice wins. The summary's condition would never trigger correctly.

- Source: "At the beginning of the game, the novices get 2 movement phases. Both are part of the first turn of the game. Move the novices twice following the rules below, and then the guards may move for their 1st turn." ✓

**Novice objective:**
- Summary: "1. Visit the location with your key, 2. Visit the location of your secret item, 3. Return to your cell."
- Source: "To win, you must first obtain the keys, then pick up your item, and then return to your cell." ✓

**Turn structure:**

- Source: "Each turn consists of 3 parts: Novices move / Guards move / Move turn marker" ✓

**Turn limit:**

- Source: "If no one has won before the end of the 15th turn, the guards win and the game ends." ✓

**Re-catching rule:**

**Line of sight limit:**
- Summary: Uses straight-edge tool (correct) ✓; does not mention 6-dot vision limit.
