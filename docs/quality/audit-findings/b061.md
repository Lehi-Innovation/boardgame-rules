## concept-kids-animals — MAJOR

The summary describes a generic Concept-style game with a rotating clue-giver and a scoring track. The actual rulebook is a cooperative kids game in which the children collectively give clues and the adult guesses, scored by a pile of found cards. Core turn structure is contradicted and components are invented.

### Findings

1. **Contradicted core mechanic — who sees the card.** Summary (Turn Structure): "The active player draws an animal card and looks at the animal without showing others." Source: "Take the top card of the deck, without looking at it, and place it in the card holder so that all kids can see it." In the base game nobody "looks secretly"; the card is displayed to all children and hidden only from the guesser (the adult).
2. **Inverted roles.** Summary: one active player gives clues; "Other players call out guesses at any time"; "The next player clockwise becomes the active player." Source base game: ALL children place rings ("taking turns, the children each place a ring") and the single adult guesses ("You can make as many guesses as you want"). A single rotating clue-giver only exists in the "For ages 6 and up" variant (which also uses 24 cards, draw two pick one — not described in the summary).
3. **Invented component/mechanic — scoring track and marker.** Summary Components list "Scoring track"; Turn Structure: "Move the scoring marker." Source contents: "1 game board • 110 cards • 16 rings • 1 card holder • 1 quick rules • This rulebook." No scoring track exists; scoring is "place the card face up next to the deck. This card will be worth 1 point at the end of the game."
4. **Setup error — deck construction.** Summary: "Shuffle the animal cards and place them face down." Source: use only blue framed cards for the first games and "randomly take 12 to form a face-down deck"; remaining cards return to the box. The 12-card deck is what ends the game.
5. **Invented setup detail.** Summary: "The youngest player starts as the active player." Not in source (the adult is the guesser by default).
6. **Unsupported claim.** Summary: "Rings can be added, removed, or rearranged at any time." Source only says children take turns placing rings; the give-up trigger is "if the children can't place any more rings" (16 rings are finite).
7. **Omission (minor).** The end-of-game rating scale ("12 = Legendary Lion … 8•9, 10•11" tiers) is omitted; summary's "measured by how many animals are guessed" only loosely covers it.

## condottiere — MAJOR

Several special cards are described with wrong effects, victory conditions are wrong for 2/5/6 players, the card-redraw structure is wrong, and a variant rule is presented as a base rule.

### Findings

1. **Wrong victory conditions (2, 5, 6 players).** Summary table: 2p = "5 adjacent"; 5p and 6p = "3 adjacent or any 4 [total]". Source: "In a game with 4, 5, or 6 players, the first player to control 5 total regions or 3 adjacent regions wins… with 2 or 3 players… 6 total regions or 4 adjacent regions." So 2p should be 6 total / 4 adjacent (summary gives no total-region option and the wrong adjacent count), and 5p/6p total should be 5, not 4. Only the 3p and 4p rows are correct.
2. **Bishop effect wrong.** Summary: "Prevents the battle from being resolved. The region remains unconquered; no one wins." Source: "When a Bishop card is played, all of the highest-strength Mercenary card(s) in play are discarded… The player who played the Bishop card then receives the Favor of the Pope token." The battle continues normally.
3. **Scarecrow effect wrong.** Summary: "retrieve all your played mercenary cards back to hand… Then you must pass." Source: "allows a player to retrieve one of his own Mercenary cards from his battle line back into his hand… he may choose not to pick up a Mercenary card." No forced pass; "all" vs "one" is contradicted.
4. **Courtesan effect wrong/garbled.** Summary: "the player with the Favour of the Pope token gains its bonus. The Courtesan then goes to a different player." Source: Courtesan is a strength-1 card; "If… a player's battle line contains the most Courtesan cards, that player receives the Condottiere token instead of the player that actually won the battle."
5. **Spring effect omitted.** Summary: "Cancels the effect of a previously played Winter card" only. Source: Spring also adds "+3 strength to each of his Mercenary cards that is among the highest-strength Mercenaries in play" — its primary battlefield effect.
6. **Variant presented as base rule.** Summary Special Rules: "A region already controlled by a player can be attacked again." Source base rule: "The Condottiere token may not be placed on a region that has the favor of the Pope or on a region that has already been conquered by another player." Attacking controlled regions is the optional "Capturing Regions" variant.
7. **Contradicted pass rule.** Summary: "If only one player remains in a battle (all others passed), that player wins regardless of strength." Source: "a player who has passed may still win the current battle, if he has the strongest battle line at the end of the battle"; the last remaining player may keep playing cards but does not auto-win.
8. **Round/redraw structure wrong.** Summary: after each battle, "each player draws back up to a hand of cards. If not, reshuffle the discard pile." Source: no drawing after battles in the base game; a round ends when all but one player (or none) is out of cards, then the deck is reshuffled and each player is dealt 10 cards plus 1 per controlled region. (Per-battle drawing is the optional "Draw After Battle" variant, with 7-card starting hands.)
9. **Omissions.** Favor of the Pope mechanics (protected region, placement on playing a Bishop), the "discard hand if no Mercenary cards" option, the most-regions endgame when the board fills, the final-battle tiebreaker, and deals/negotiation rules are all absent. Components omit "17 regions" and "36 control markers in 6 different colors."
10. **Setup detail wrong (minor).** Summary: "The starting player is chosen randomly." Source: "The youngest player takes the Condottiere token… deals 10 cards… places the Condottiere token in a region of his choice" and takes the first turn.

## conflict-of-heroes-awakening-the-bear-russia-1941-42 — MAJOR

The 7-AP activation core and CAP economy are captured, but the action cost table contains wrong/invented values, combat resolution is misdescribed via an invented "Hit Table", an "Assault Move" action is invented, and the stacking claim directly contradicts the source.

### Findings

1. **Wrong fire cost.** Summary Actions table: "Fire | 1 AP". Source: the AP cost to fire is printed per unit ("The black number in the top left corner of the counter shows the number of APs the unit must spend to attack"); examples: "it costs 2APs to fire or 1AP to move", "Rifle A fires… paying its 3AP cost", "Rifle C must pay 4APs to fire". No unit fires for 1 AP in the examples.
2. **Wrong rally cost.** Summary: "Rally | 1 AP". Source 7.6: "The cost to attempt a rally action is 5APs."
3. **Invented action.** Summary: "Assault Move | 2 AP | Move and fire in the same activation." No such action exists in the source (the word "assault" appears only descriptively). Movement and fire are separate AP actions; close combat (7.7.3) is the move-into-hex attack system, which works differently.
4. **Invented combat procedure ("Hit Table").** Summary Fire Resolution: "Subtract the defender's defense value and terrain modifiers. Roll 2d6… Compare to the Hit Table: results range from no effect to eliminated." Source 7.0–7.3: AV = unit FP + 2D6 (+ up to 2 CAPs); DV = DR + terrain DM; "If the AV is equal to or greater than the DV, the defender takes one hit. Critical Hit. If the AV is greater than the DV by 4 or more, the defender is immediately destroyed." On a hit you randomly draw a hit marker (stunned, pinned, suppressed, KIA, etc.) — there is no graded Hit Table. The summary also omits critical hits and the random hit-marker draw entirely.
5. **Contradicted stacking rule.** Summary: "Stacking limits apply (typically 2 units per hex)." Source (5.0 and 7.5.1, stated twice): "There are no stacking limits."
6. **Misdescribed turn alternation.** Summary: "After the active unit completes actions (or the player passes), the opponent activates a unit." Source 2.2: players alternate turns where each turn is ONE action; an activated unit's AP actions are interspersed with the opponent's turns ("Each individual shared action still counts as a turn"), and both players may have an activated unit at the same time. The summary's "finish a unit, then opponent goes" framing would change the game fundamentally. It also omits the per-round 2D6 initiative roll (2.1) and the stall option.
7. **Opportunity fire misframed (minor within the above).** Summary: "0 AP (reaction)… during the opponent's activation." Source 3.1: an opportunity action is taken on your own turn with any fresh unit, costs no APs/CAPs, and marks the unit spent. The 0-cost/spent part is right; "reaction during the opponent's activation" is not how it works.
8. **Counter layout reference mostly wrong.** Summary: "Attack Value (top left), Defense Value (top right), Movement (bottom right), Range (bottom left)." Source: top left = AP cost to fire; top right = movement cost (foot units); lower left = firepower; lower right = defense ratings; range = "bottom center of the counter inside the country symbol".
9. **Vehicles armor (minor).** Summary: "front/side/rear armor values." Source: units have a front DR and a flank DR only (flank covers side/rear).
10. **Supported items.** 7 APs per activation ("it receives 7 action points (APs)"); CAPs per scenario, shared across a player's units, reset each round (minus losses); both players passing consecutively ends the round; spent units flip back to fresh; VPs from destroyed units (immediately, per unit VP value) and victory hex control; flank attacks resolved against weaker flank DR; suppression; programmed instruction; 2D6.

## connect-four — MAJOR

The summary documents the wrong product. The extracted source is "Connect 4 & More" (Winning Moves), a tabletop TILE game: "CONTENTS: 40 game tiles and instructions," "For 2 or 4 PLAYERS," with rules for six different games (Classic, Backwards, Teams, Connect Three-4, Any Color, & More & More & More). The summary instead describes the classic vertical-grid Connect Four, none of whose components or mechanics appear in the source.

### Findings

1. **Components unsupported.** Summary: "1 vertical grid (6 rows x 7 columns), 2 end supports, 21 black checkers, 21 red checkers." Source: "40 game tiles and instructions" — no grid, no supports, no checkers, and the colors are red and yellow ("One player is the 'red' player… the other is the 'yellow' player"), not black/red.
2. **Setup/mechanics unsupported.** Sliding lever, dropping checkers into 7 slots, checkers falling to the lowest position, full columns, clearing the board with the lever — none of this is in the source. Source play: players alternately place face-up tiles on the table, matching edge colors of all adjacent tiles ("Whenever a tile is placed it must match all edges of all adjacent tiles"); an unplayable tile goes to the bottom of the draw pile and the turn is lost.
3. **Major omission.** All six games in the source are omitted: Classic Connect 4 (tiles), Backwards Connect 4 (first to 4-in-a-row LOSES), Connect 4 Teams (4 players, ten tiles each), Connect Three-4 (first to four rows of three wins), Connect 4 Any Color, and Connect 4 & More & More & More (1 point per row of 4, most points wins).
4. **Unsupported edge rules.** "The player who starts the first game will play second in the next game" and "If the grid is completely filled… the game is a draw" — neither appears in the source.
5. **Player count.** Frontmatter says "2"; the source product is "For 2 or 4 PLAYERS" (Teams variant is 4-player).
6. **Supported items (incidental).** "The youngest player goes first" (source: "The youngest player goes first"); win by 4-in-a-row "in a horizontal, vertical or diagonal line."
